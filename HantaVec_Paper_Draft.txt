HantaVec: Cross-Segment Evolutionary Analysis of Orthohantavirus
Using ESM-2 Protein Language Model Embeddings

ABSTRACT

Background: Orthohantaviruses possess a tripartite negative-sense RNA genome encoding distinct proteins across three segments (S, M, L), yet most phylogenetic analyses focus on single segments. Cross-segment evolutionary consistency remains understudied despite its importance for understanding viral evolution and recombination events.

Methods: We analyzed 767 high-quality orthohantavirus sequences across all three genome segments using ESM-2 protein language model embeddings. Our dataset included 159 Seoul, 259 Andes, 152 Puumala, 60 Prospect Hill, 69 Choclo, and 42 Sin Nombre virus sequences. Cross-segment evolutionary consistency was assessed through matched isolate analysis and cosine similarity metrics.

Results: ESM-2 embeddings successfully captured orthohantavirus evolutionary relationships with clear species separation (UMAP) and robust clade differentiation (Old World: n=373, New World: n=394). Cross-segment analysis of 126 matched isolates revealed high evolutionary consistency: S-L (0.968 ± 0.113), S-M (0.931 ± 0.131), and M-L (0.875 ± 0.234). Principal component analysis showed 92.5% variance explained by PC1, indicating strong phylogenetic signal preservation in protein embeddings.

Conclusions: This represents the first comprehensive three-segment orthohantavirus analysis using protein language models. High cross-segment consistency validates the utility of ESM-2 embeddings for viral phylogenetics and suggests minimal recombination in major orthohantavirus lineages. The approach enables rapid, alignment-free evolutionary analysis suitable for outbreak surveillance and zoonotic risk assessment.

Keywords: Orthohantavirus, ESM-2, protein language models, viral evolution, cross-segment analysis, phylogenetics

INTRODUCTION

Orthohantaviruses are segmented negative-sense RNA viruses of the family Hantaviridae, responsible for hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS) and hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) in humans. The viral genome consists of three segments encoding nucleocapsid protein (S segment), glycoproteins (M segment), and RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (L segment). This tripartite architecture enables reassortment events that can drive rapid viral evolution and emergence of novel variants.

Traditional phylogenetic approaches typically analyze single segments, most commonly the partial S segment due to historical sequencing limitations. However, single-segment analyses may miss important evolutionary signals and recombination events that occur between segments. Comprehensive three-segment analysis requires computationally intensive multiple sequence alignments and is often limited by sequence availability.

Recent advances in protein language models, particularly ESM-2 (Evolutionary Scale Modeling), offer new approaches for analyzing viral evolution. These models capture evolutionary relationships through learned representations of protein sequences without requiring explicit alignments. ESM-2 has demonstrated success in protein structure prediction and functional annotation, but its application to viral phylogenetics and cross-segment evolutionary analysis remains unexplored.

Here we present HantaVec, a comprehensive three-segment analysis of orthohantavirus evolution using ESM-2 embeddings. Our approach enables rapid, alignment-free analysis of cross-segment evolutionary consistency and provides insights into orthohantavirus phylogenetic relationships across major lineages.

METHODS

Dataset Construction
We compiled a comprehensive dataset of 767 orthohantavirus protein sequences spanning all three genome segments from public databases. Sequences were classified by species using standardized nomenclature and segment assignment was performed using extraction method ground truth mapping. Quality control excluded partial sequences <50 amino acids and removed near-duplicates within segments while preserving cross-segment matches.

ESM-2 Embedding Generation
Protein sequences were processed using the ESM-2 12-layer, 35M parameter model with 1022 amino acid maximum length. Sequences exceeding this limit were truncated from the C-terminus. Embeddings were generated through mean pooling of hidden states excluding special tokens. The final embedding dataset achieved 100% coverage with zero zero-vectors, ensuring high-quality representations for all sequences.

Cross-Segment Analysis
Matched isolates were identified by combining species and isolate metadata across segments. Cross-segment evolutionary consistency was quantified using cosine similarity between embeddings of the same isolate across different segments. Statistical analysis included variance decomposition and correlation analysis.

Dimensionality Reduction and Visualization
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was performed on the full 480-dimensional embeddings. UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) was applied to 50-dimensional PCA coordinates for 2D visualization. Species clustering and clade separation were assessed using convex hull analysis and inter-centroid distances.

RESULTS

Dataset Characteristics
Our final dataset comprised 767 sequences distributed across three segments: S (274 sequences), M (244 sequences), and L (249 sequences). Species representation included major pathogenic lineages: Andes (259), Seoul (159), Puumala (152), Prospect Hill (60), Choclo (69), and Sin Nombre (42). Sequence lengths ranged from 52 to 6,530 amino acids, with L-segment sequences requiring complete truncation to 1022 amino acids.

Cross-Segment Evolutionary Consistency
Analysis of 126 matched isolates spanning 10 species revealed 302 pairwise cross-segment comparisons. Evolutionary consistency was highest between S-L segments (0.968 ± 0.113, n=90), followed by S-M (0.931 ± 0.131, n=104) and M-L (0.875 ± 0.234, n=108). Andes virus dominated the matched isolate set (66 isolates, 178 pairs), providing robust statistical power for consistency analysis.

Species and Clade Separation
UMAP visualization demonstrated clear species-level clustering with distinct convex hulls for major lineages. PCA analysis showed strong phylogenetic signal preservation with 92.5% variance explained by the first principal component. Old World (n=373) and New World (n=394) clades exhibited distinct clustering with inter-centroid distance of 0.76 UMAP units, confirming known biogeographic patterns.

Embedding Quality Assessment
ESM-2 embeddings achieved perfect coverage (767/767 sequences) with zero zero-vectors, indicating robust model performance across diverse orthohantavirus lineages. Mean embedding norms were consistent across segments (S: 6.69, M: 6.78, L: 6.92), suggesting uniform representation quality despite sequence length variations.

DISCUSSION

This study represents the first comprehensive three-segment orthohantavirus analysis using protein language models. Our results demonstrate that ESM-2 embeddings successfully capture evolutionary relationships across diverse orthohantavirus lineages while enabling rapid, alignment-free analysis.

The high cross-segment evolutionary consistency (all correlations >0.87) suggests minimal reassortment in major orthohantavirus lineages, consistent with co-evolution of viral proteins within host-adapted lineages. The lower M-L consistency (0.875) compared to S-L (0.968) may reflect functional constraints on glycoprotein evolution or differential selection pressures.

Clear species and clade separation in embedding space validates the phylogenetic signal preservation in ESM-2 representations. The strong Old World/New World dichotomy (inter-centroid distance 0.76) reflects known biogeographic patterns and supports the utility of this approach for epidemiological applications.

Limitations include potential truncation effects for L-segment analysis and representation bias toward well-studied species. Future work should address these limitations and extend analysis to additional hantavirus genera.

CONCLUSIONS

HantaVec demonstrates the utility of protein language models for comprehensive viral phylogenetic analysis. The approach enables rapid cross-segment consistency assessment and species-level classification without computational intensive alignments. High evolutionary consistency across segments supports the reliability of single-segment analyses for orthohantavirus phylogenetics while providing new tools for outbreak investigation and zoonotic risk assessment.
